# The 1×5×3 Content System

The complete system Hiruno V2 implements. All skills in this repo reference this doc as the single source of truth.

## The core equation

**1 topic × 5 hooks × 3 formats = 15 posts**

One topic gets you about three weeks of content posting five times per week. Two topics gets you a daily posting plan for a month. The whole system runs on this multiplication.

## The North Star metric: skip rate

Instagram's leaked ranking signals confirmed it. **Skip rate is the number-one ranking signal** for reach. Skip rate is the percent of viewers who scroll within the first three seconds. Low skip rate equals strong hook equals more reach.

This means the agent is engineered around one job: stop the scroll in three seconds, then keep them watching. Every skill in this repo serves that job.

## Layer 1: the two-part architecture

Every post has two parts. Optimise them separately.

- **Message** — what you say
- **Format** — how you package it

A brilliant message in a bad format dies. A clean format with empty content is filler. Both must work.

## Layer 2: the messaging principle (doctor vs patient)

Speak to the symptom, not the diagnosis. Patients describe how they feel. Doctors name the condition. Most creators talk like the doctor when their audience is the patient.

Audit test: does this line sound like terminology, or does it sound like how a real person describes their own pain or desire? If it's terminology, rewrite.

Examples:

- Diagnosis: "Five evidence-based interventions for circadian rhythm optimisation"
- Symptom: "Why you feel exhausted by 2pm even when you slept eight hours"

- Diagnosis: "Cognitive reframing protocols for restructuring maladaptive thought patterns"
- Symptom: "Why can't you celebrate a win for more than five minutes before chasing the next thing"

Show the expertise inside the post. Lead with the feeling.

## Layer 3: the five hooks

These are the only five. Stop collecting more.

### 1. Ask a question

Open a curiosity gap by making the viewer mentally try to answer.

Example: "When was the last time you did something that actually scared you?"

Pro tip: combine the question with calling out a specific demographic. "Should athletes eat pasta or rice before a game?" pulls in every athlete and the algorithm pushes it to more athletes.

### 2. Numbered list

Numbers feel safe and trackable. The brain wants to know what is on the list, especially the first item.

Example: "Here are my top five most useful subreddits, from someone who hit financial freedom in her twenties."

Pro tip: make item one the most controversial or surprising. It re-hooks the viewer right after the title hook so they don't bail at point two.

### 3. Tell a story

The brain refuses to abandon a story before it resolves.

Example: "I had a C-section four weeks ago, and the number of people I know who have had one and acted like it's no big deal is one million."

Pro tip: do not reveal the resolution in the hook. Set the stakes, leave the ending hidden.

### 4. Negative hook

Loss aversion is twice as strong as desire. Fear of missing the right thing beats curiosity about the right thing.

Example: "Never post on Instagram again without turning on these three settings."

Pro tip: carry the negative framing through the body. If the hook is "stop doing these five things," each item should start with "don't."

### 5. Contrarian view

Take a widely held belief and flip it. The viewer has to either agree or disagree, and either way they keep watching.

Example: "Stop following your passion, it's keeping you broke."

Pro tip: pair it with a visual that is equally surprising. The double take buys you another two seconds before they decide whether to swipe.

### The hook qualifying test

For every hook you write, ask: **does this make someone need to know what comes next?** If the answer is anything less than yes, rewrite.

## Layer 4: the three formats

Three packaging options. Pick one based on skill and time, not preference.

### Direct to camera

Phone, talking head. Roughly 30 minutes to make. Highest converting for new followers because the viewer is buying personality, not just information.

Best for: people who like talking.

Production trick: do not memorise. Record one line at a time, then cut the dead space. Use CapCut or Instagram Edits.

### Storytelling carousel

Canva, free version. Roughly 15 minutes. Viewer controls pace, which is why it converts well for written-heavy ideas. Each slide gets one title and one takeaway. Do not bombard a slide with text.

Best for: people who write better than they talk.

### B-roll plus text

One video clip with a hook on screen, value lives in the caption. Roughly 10 minutes. The reason it works is mechanical: reading the caption takes longer than the clip is long, so the clip loops while the viewer reads. Average watch time spikes, retention goes up, the algorithm pushes it.

Best for: visual products and busy creators.

## Layer 5: the three format parameters

Inside whichever format, three things must be true:

- **Strong hook**. One of the five above. Three-second test.
- **Real value**. Education, relatability, or a takeaway. If you cannot finish the sentence "the viewer will leave knowing or feeling X," the post is selfish.
- **Re-hooks**. Structural turns inside the post that re-engage. Numbered titles inside a carousel. Contrast pairs ("being lonely versus being alone"). New text on screen at the four-second mark of a B-roll. Without re-hooks, watch time drops at the first dead beat.

## How the system runs end to end

1. **Pick one topic** from a brand pillar.
2. **Write five hooks** for that topic, one per type. Apply the symptom-first rule. Run the qualifying test.
3. **Pick a format for each hook**. Mix them or duplicate. Either way you end with up to 15 distinct posts from one topic.
4. **Build a posting grid**. One topic gets ~3 weeks at 5x/week. Two topics gets daily for a month.
5. **Isolate variables on ship**. Keep format constant for a week to learn hook craft fast. Keep hook constant and change format to learn what your audience prefers to consume.

## The why anchor

Performance is dictated by perspective. The way you view what you do impacts how you do what you do. When the why is strong, the how becomes easier.

This matters mechanically: most creators quit at post 5, 6, 7 because they don't see traction. The inflection comes later. The why is what carries you through the dip. The brand context file captures the why so the agent can write content that sounds like it actually means something to whoever is publishing it.
