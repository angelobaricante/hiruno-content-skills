# Hook Pro Tips

The small moves that turn an OK hook into a high-retention hook.

## Universal pro tips (apply to all hook types)

### Call out the demographic

Combine the hook with a specific demographic call-out. The algorithm pushes the post to more of that demographic, and the people in that demographic feel personally addressed.

- "Should athletes eat pasta or rice" — pulls athletes
- "Things I tell my 21-year-old self as a 24-year-old" — pulls 18 to 26 year olds
- "If you've yelled at your kid this week and you hated yourself the second after" — pulls parents who feel guilty

### Specificity over generality

A specific number, age, time, scenario, or place beats general claims every time.

- "Why you feel exhausted by 2pm" beats "Why you feel exhausted in the afternoon"
- "5 months traveling around Europe" beats "after travelling for a while"
- "33-year-old with good money skills" beats "someone good with money"

### Lead with the symptom, not the diagnosis

This is the universal rule. Patients describe what they feel. Doctors name conditions. The audience is the patient.

## Question hook pro tips

### Pro tip: combine the question with calling out a demographic

This is the highest-leverage move for the question hook. It pulls a specific audience and the algorithm distributes accordingly.

Example: "Should athletes eat pasta or rice before their game?" calls out athletes by name.

### Pro tip: ask a question the viewer already has but hasn't articulated

The strongest questions are ones the viewer has thought about but never put into words. When you put their unspoken question into words, they feel seen and stay to find your answer.

Example: "Why does your team always agree with you in meetings, even when you know they shouldn't?"

## Numbered list pro tips

### Pro tip: make item one the most controversial

The first listed item serves as a re-hook right after the title hook. If item one is boring, the viewer bails after the first beat. If item one is the most surprising or contrarian thing on the list, they stay for the whole thing.

Example: in "5 things I refuse to spend money on as a 33-year-old who's good with money", lead with the most counter-intuitive (e.g. "buying a phone with a payment plan") not the obvious (e.g. "designer clothes").

### Pro tip: pick odd numbers

3, 5, 7, 9 outperform even numbers slightly. Reads as more curated, less arbitrary.

### Pro tip: tie the list to a specific identity

"5 reasons your consistency is hurting your gym routine" is good. "5 reasons your consistency is hurting your gym routine if you're under 5'4 and an apartment lifter" is better.

## Story hook pro tips

### Pro tip: do not reveal the resolution in the hook

The mistake is saying "I lost 50 pounds and here's how." That kills the curiosity gap by giving away the ending.

Strong: "I lost 50 pounds, then gained 10 back, and it put me in a spiral."
Weak: "I lost 50 pounds and I want to share my method."

The strong version sets up the question (does it get worse, or does she fix it?). The weak version answers the question before the viewer asks.

### Pro tip: open with stakes, not setup

Skip the scene-setting. Open with the moment that matters most.

Strong: "I had a C-section four weeks ago, and the number of people I know who have had one and acted like it's no big deal is one million."
Weak: "Last month I had a baby. It was via C-section. I want to talk about my recovery."

### Pro tip: use real numbers and real names

Stories with specific details (a real number, a real time, a real place, a real person's name) feel true. Stories with fuzzy details feel made up.

## Negative hook pro tips

### Pro tip: carry the negative through the body

If the hook is "stop doing these five things," each of the five points should start with "don't." The matched language compounds the negative pressure across the whole post.

Example: from the Daily Stoic post:
- Hook: "The Stoics say you need to stop doing these five things"
- Body item 1: "Don't suffer imagined troubles"
- Body item 2: "Don't argue with people who'll never agree"
- (etc, all starting with "don't")

### Pro tip: be specific about what they'll lose

"Never post on Instagram again without turning on these three settings" works because the loss is specific (good Instagram performance) and the fix is specific (three settings).

"Don't make this mistake" is too vague. What mistake? What's lost?

### Pro tip: use loss aversion language

Words that pull weight: "never", "stop", "don't", "before", "without", "you'll regret", "the reason you're not", "what's killing your".

## Contrarian hook pro tips

### Pro tip: pair with an equally surprising visual

The contrarian view is verbal. The visual should be surprising too. The double take buys an extra two to three seconds.

Example: Emily's "you need to be playing dumb at work, like stupid" was paired with her cutting bananas with scissors into a glass. The visual makes you stop scrolling before you even hear the audio.

### Pro tip: make sure the take is genuinely contrarian, not just edgy

The test: would 70% of your audience disagree with the hook on first read? If yes, it's a real contrarian view. If most people would already agree (e.g. "money isn't everything"), it's not contrarian, it's a platitude.

### Pro tip: have the proof ready

Contrarian hooks set up an argument the body has to win. If you flip a belief in the hook and don't deliver evidence in the body, the post feels hollow. Before writing a contrarian hook, make sure you have the receipts to back the take.

### Pro tip: pick a belief the audience has, not one you have

The belief you're flipping should be one your specific audience holds. "Stop following your passion" is contrarian to ambitious 20-somethings. It's not contrarian to seasoned professionals who already learned that lesson.
